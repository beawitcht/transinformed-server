Why is a formal diagnosis recommended?

A formal diagnosis is recommended as most GPs will want this before they will agree to providing gender affirming care.
A formal diagnosis is not necessary for a bridging prescription.

Why is a recommendation letter recommended?

This letter is provided by a gender specialist and will contain the details of what hormone treatment is being recommended, this is what the doctor will prescribe based off.

What I need from my GP

Why is a shared care agreement recommended?

A shared care agreement is recommended if you are able to afford assistance from a private service, as this will allow you to receive HRT treatment from a specialist, without a lengthy GIC referral.

Which Gender Identity Clinic is right for me?

All GICs have requirements on who can be referred to them (based on age and location, and if they're accepting referrals). Check out this resource from Gender Construction Kit to make sure the GIC in question is the right choice for you.

Which private provider is right for me?

When choosing a private provider, GPs are much more likely to work with a specialist who is based in the UK, and registered with the GMC. Check out this resource from Gender Construction Kit to help decide on the service that is right for you.

Why is the bridging prescription greyed out?

If you need a bridging prescription, the GMC and NHS require that you are self-prescribing, or likely to self prescribe.

Why is a bridging prescription not recommended?

A bridging prescription is prescribed as "the lowest acceptable dose" and this tends to be too low of a dose to be effective.

How can I continue with HRT when migrating to the UK?

It is highly recommended that you obtain a letter from your previous HRT provider, if you need help deciding the contents of this letter, check out this letter template.
